## Scenario 1: The Diplomatic Crisis

### The Situation

Two nations at the brink of war. Neither can back down without losing face. Any statement in their languages commits to a position. The translator proposes: speak in Limn.

### The Exchange

**Nation A's representative:**
```limn
we wan pea | we pre str | you act det wh
```
*We want peace. We prepare strength. Your action determines which.*

**Nation B's representative:**
```limn
sam | we wan pea | we pre str | bot act det | pea str sam ori
```
*Same. We want peace. We prepare strength. Both actions determine. Peace and strength: same origin.*

**The Resolution:**
```limn
bot wan sam | bot fea dif | fea fal > pea ris
```
*Both want same. Both fear different. When fear falls, peace rises.*

### Why Limn Matters

In English, "We want peace but are prepared for war" sounds like a threat. The "but" creates opposition.

In Limn, `we wan pea | we pre str` holds both without opposition. The scope operator `|` places them side by side, not in conflict. Both are true. Neither undermines the other.

The phrase `pea str sam ori` - "peace and strength same origin" - cannot be said in diplomatic English without sounding like doublespeak. In Limn, it's a geometric fact: both words define regions that share territory (courage, resolve, commitment).

## Scenario 2: The Grief Counselor

### The Situation

A person has lost someone. They cannot articulate their state - it's not sadness exactly, not anger, not numbness. It's all of these and none. The counselor offers Limn.

### The Exchange

**Mourner:**
```limn
fee | nu fee | fee nu fee | emp ful | sel nu sel
```

**Counselor:**
```limn
und | fee cor | wor nu fit | lim fit
```
*I understand. Feeling is correct. Words don't fit. Limn fits.*

**Mourner:**
```limn
lov her | los her | lov los | los lov | her nu her | sel nu sel
```
*Loved them. Lost them. Love is loss. Loss is love. They are not-them. I am not-me.*

**Counselor:**
```limn
gri | gri lim | bou bet lif dea | you sta yon bou | dur
```
*Grief. Grief is liminal. The boundary between life and death. You stand at that boundary. Duration.*

### Why Limn Matters

Grief is not an emotion but a superposition of emotions. English forces selection: "I feel sad" or "I feel angry" or "I feel numb." The mourner must betray their experience to speak it.

`fee | nu fee | fee nu fee` - Feeling, not-feeling, feeling-not-feeling. This IS grief. The contradictions aren't resolved - they're held.

`her nu her` - They are not-them. The loved one exists and doesn't. Memory is presence and absence simultaneously.

The counselor's validation: `wor nu fit | lim fit` - words don't fit, Limn fits. Permission to feel in superposition.

## Scenario 3: The Code Review

### The Situation

A senior developer reviews a junior's code. The code works but something is wrong. In English, every critique sounds like attack. In Limn, observation and judgment can separate.

### The Exchange

**Senior:**
```limn
cod wor | cod nu cle | wor nu imp goo
```
*Code works. Code not-clear. Working doesn't imply good.*

**Junior:**
```limn
und | wh nu cle?
```
*Understand. What is not-clear?*

**Senior:**
```limn
fun A | do man | nec one | sho div
```
*Function A does many things. Needs to be one. Should divide.*

**Junior:**
```limn
see | A > B C D | B do X | C do Y | D do Z | cle?
```
*I see. A becomes B, C, D. B does X. C does Y. D does Z. Clear?*

**Senior:**
```limn
yes | goo lea | cod tra | you tra | sam
```
*Yes. Good learning. Code transforms. You transform. Same.*

### Why Limn Matters

Code review in English is fraught. "Your code is unclear" sounds like "You are unclear." The person and work blur.

In Limn, `cod nu cle` is a statement about the code's constraint region, not about the person. The junior can receive it as geometric information.

The senior's closing: `cod tra | you tra | sam` - code transforms, you transform, same process. The refactoring and the learning are identified as the same operation. Growth, not criticism.

## Scenario 4: The Therapy Session

### The Situation

A patient struggles to describe a recurring pattern. They do something, regret it, do it again. In English, they can only narrate the sequence. In Limn, they can describe the structure.

### The Exchange

**Patient:**
```limn
sel do X | sel hat X | sel do X | cyc | nu und wh
```
*Self does X. Self hates X. Self does X. Cycle. Don't understand why.*

**Therapist:**
```limn
X | wh X giv?
```
*X - what does X give?*

**Patient:**
```limn
X giv cal | bre | X end > cal end > nec X
```
*X gives calm. Brief. X ends, calm ends, need X.*

**Therapist:**
```limn
see pat | X nu pro | X sym | cal nec und | und > new pat pos
```
*I see the pattern. X is not the problem. X is symptom. Calm-need requires understanding. Understanding enables new patterns.*

**Patient:**
```limn
wh cal nec? | wh nu cal bad?
```
*Why do I need calm? Why is not-calm bad?*

**Therapist:**
```limn
goo que | nu cal | wh fee? | ori whe?
```
*Good question. Not-calm - what feeling? Origin where?*

### Why Limn Matters

Addiction/compulsion patterns are cycles, not narratives. English forces "First I do X, then I feel bad, then I do X again" - a sequence that implies the person could simply stop at step 2.

`sel do X | sel hat X | sel do X | cyc` - The cycle is one structure, not three events. The pattern exists whole.

`X giv cal | bre | X end > cal end > nec X` - The causal chain is visible as geometry. X gives brief calm; when X ends, calm ends, creating need for X. The trap is structural.

The therapist's insight: `X nu pro | X sym` - X is not the problem, X is symptom. In English, this sounds dismissive. In Limn, it's a constraint reframing: we're not looking at X's region, we're looking at the region that generates need for X.

## Scenario 5: The Art Critique

### The Situation

An artist presents work. Critics must respond. In English, every statement takes a position. In Limn, response can remain in superposition with the work.

### The Exchange

**Artist presents work. No words.**

**Critic 1:**
```limn
see | mov | mov nu exp | fee unk | goo
```
*I see. It moves me. The movement I can't explain. Feeling unknown. Good.*

**Critic 2:**
```limn
see | nu und | nu und goo | mys val
```
*I see. Don't understand. Not-understanding is good. Mystery has value.*

**Critic 3:**
```limn
see | for ref | tra for | ref nu los | new
```
*I see. Form references tradition. Transforms tradition. Reference not lost. New.*

**Artist:**
```limn
tha | all tru | non int | wor ope | key you
```
*Thanks. All true. None intended. Work is open. Key is yours.*

### Why Limn Matters

Art criticism in English forces interpretation. "The work represents X" or "The artist is saying Y." The critic must claim to know what the work means.

In Limn, `see | mov | mov nu exp` - seeing, being moved, the movement unexplainable - this IS the critique. No claim about meaning. Pure response.

The artist's reply: `all tru | non int | wor ope` - all readings true, none intended, work is open. This isn't modesty or evasion. It's a statement about the work's nature. Multiple valid collapses; the artist doesn't privilege one.

`key you` - The key is yours. The work exists in superposition. Each viewer collapses it differently. All collapses are valid.

## Scenario 6: The Conflict Mediation

### The Situation

Two parties in dispute. Each has a narrative. The narratives conflict. A mediator uses Limn to find the shared region.

### The Exchange

**Party A:**
```limn
sel do goo | oth do bad | oth cau har | sel vic
```
*I did good. They did bad. They caused harm. I am victim.*

**Party B:**
```limn
sel do goo | oth do bad | oth cau har | sel vic
```
*I did good. They did bad. They caused harm. I am victim.*

**Mediator:**
```limn
see | bot say sam | bot fee sam | bot exp dif | key dif | eve sam
```
*I see. Both say the same. Both feel the same. Both experience differently. Keys differ. Events same.*

**Both parties:**
```limn
nu pos | we dif | we nu sam
```
*Not possible. We are different. We are not the same.*

**Mediator:**
```limn
you dif | wor sam | fee sam | bot wan und | bot wan see | yo sha reg
```
*You are different. Words same. Feelings same. Both want understanding. Both want to be seen. This is shared region.*

### Why Limn Matters

Conflict narratives in English are zero-sum. If I'm right, you're wrong. If I'm the victim, you're the aggressor.

The mediator's insight: both parties use IDENTICAL Limn: `sel do goo | oth do bad | oth cau har | sel vic`. The structure is symmetric. This would be obscured in English where different word choices would make the narratives seem different.

`bot say sam | bot fee sam | bot exp dif | key dif | eve sam` - Both say same, both feel same, both experience differently, keys differ, events same. The mediator separates the layers: shared language, shared emotion, different experience, different interpretive key, same underlying events.

The shared region: `bot wan und | bot wan see` - both want understanding, both want to be seen. This is where work can begin.

## Scenario 7: The Teaching Moment

### The Situation

A teacher must explain something the student isn't ready to understand. In English, either oversimplify or overwhelm. In Limn, offer a structure that can unfold.

### The Exchange

**Student:**
```limn
wh lif? | wh mea? | wh poi?
```
*What is life? What is meaning? What is the point?*

**Teacher:**
```limn
big que | ans gro | now sma ans | lat big ans | sam que
```
*Big questions. Answers grow. Now: small answer. Later: big answer. Same questions.*

**Student:**
```limn
say now ans
```
*Tell me the now-answer.*

**Teacher:**
```limn
lif | mov tra dur | mea | you giv | poi | que sel
```
*Life: movement, transformation, duration. Meaning: you give it. Point: the question itself.*

**Student:**
```limn
nu sat | wan mor
```
*Not satisfied. Want more.*

**Teacher:**
```limn
goo | wan mor | ans gro | que sta | you cha | ans cha
```
*Good. Wanting more - answers grow. Questions stay. You change. Answers change.*

### Why Limn Matters

The existential questions can't be answered satisfyingly in English. Any answer sounds either trite or evasive.

The teacher's strategy: `now sma ans | lat big ans | sam que` - small answer now, big answer later, same questions. This isn't stalling - it's describing how understanding actually grows. The questions don't change; the questioner does.

`poi | que sel` - The point is the question itself. In English, this sounds like a dodge. In Limn, it's structural: the constraint region defined by "what is the point?" IS the point. The asking is the answer.

The student's dissatisfaction (`nu sat | wan mor`) is validated: `goo | wan mor` - Good, wanting more. Dissatisfaction with incomplete answers is correct response.

## Meta-Commentary: Why These Scenarios?

### Common Thread

Each scenario involves communication that English distorts:
- **Diplomacy:** Positions harden in natural language
- **Grief:** Contradictory feelings forced into sequence
- **Code review:** Person and work blur
- **Therapy:** Cycles flatten into narratives
- **Art:** Interpretation colonizes experience
- **Mediation:** Symmetric conflicts appear asymmetric
- **Teaching:** Deep questions get shallow answers

### What Limn Offers

| English Problem | Limn Solution |
|-----------------|---------------|
| Either/or framing | Simultaneous holding |
| Temporal sequence | Structural relationships |
| Speaker attribution | Scope separation |
| Judgment embedded | Observation separated |
| Single meaning | Multiple valid collapses |
| Position commitment | Region description |

### The Deeper Pattern

Limn matters when the truth is superpositional.

Human experience often involves:
- Contradictory emotions
- Symmetric conflicts
- Cyclic patterns
- Multiple valid interpretations
- Truths that become false when spoken

English, evolved for coordination and action, forces collapse. "What do you want?" "Where do you stand?" "What do you mean?"

Limn preserves the superposition. It lets us speak from the space before choice. Sometimes that's what's needed.
