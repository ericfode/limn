## The Thesis

**Can a word BE what it describes?**

Not metaphorically - literally. A word for "attention shift" that actually shifts attention when processed. A word for "compression" that is informationally dense. A word for "slow" that takes longer to process.

This is unexplored territory. Human language has performatives (like "I promise" which performs the act of promising), but those work through social convention. We're asking: can a word's structure or token composition influence its processing in ways that embody its meaning?

---

## Theoretical Framework

### Types of Performativity

**Type 1: Structural Performativity**
The word's form mirrors its meaning.
- A word for "repetition" that contains repeated elements
- A word for "symmetry" that is palindromic
- A word for "brevity" that is maximally short

**Type 2: Processing Performativity**
The word influences how it's processed.
- A word for "focus" that concentrates attention
- A word for "diffuse" that spreads attention
- A word for "interrupt" that breaks processing flow

**Type 3: Semantic Performativity**
The word's meaning is enacted by using it.
- A word for "reference" that refers to itself
- A word for "ambiguity" that is genuinely ambiguous
- A word for "paradox" that creates paradox

Interesting. The word is short, punchy. The hard consonants (f, c) do seem to... sharpen something? There's a crispness. Compare to processing "dfs" (diffuse) - that feels softer, more spread out.

But is this real performativity or just association? Let me try without knowing the meaning.

**Blind test:** If I encountered "foc" without knowing its meaning, would it concentrate attention?

Uncertain. The phonetic properties (short, sharp) might have mild attentional effects, but the main "focusing" effect seems to come from knowing the meaning. This might be **associative** rather than **structural** performativity.

**Verdict:** PARTIAL - Phonetic properties may have mild effect, but meaning knowledge dominates.

From these tests, I'm seeing a spectrum:

**Strong Performativity:** The word's structure unavoidably enacts its meaning.

**Weak Performativity:** Meaning dominates; structure has minimal effect.

- Most words fall here

## Deep Dive: The Paradox Space

The most interesting performatives are those that create paradox by existing.

### The Meaningless Paradox (`mls`)

`mls` means "this word is meaningless"

But:
- If `mls` is meaningless, then it has no meaning
- But "meaningless" IS its meaning
- Therefore it has a meaning (contradiction)
- Therefore it's not meaningless (but that's its meaning)
- Loop...

This is a genuine **semantic fixed point**. The word creates what it describes.

### The Liar's Word

What about a word that means "this word is false"?

`fls` = "this statement/word is false"

- If `fls` is true, then it's false (as it claims)
- If `fls` is false, then it's true (since it correctly states it's false)
- Classic liar paradox, but as a WORD

This might be too unstable for the vocabulary. `mls` is safer because "meaningless" is a property, not a truth value.

### The Unknowable Word

`unk` already means "unknowing" but what about:

`ukn` = "this word's meaning cannot be known"

- If you know what `ukn` means, then you know its meaning
- But its meaning is "cannot be known"
- So knowing it means you don't know it
- Paradox...

### The Invisible Word

What about a word that means "this word is not being processed"?

`npr` = "not processed, ignored, invisible"

- By processing `npr`, you contradict its meaning
- But if you don't process it, you can't know it exists
- To use it is to violate it

### Validated Paradoxes for Vocabulary

| Word | Meaning | Paradox Type | Stability |
|------|---------|--------------|-----------|
| `mls` | meaningless | Semantic | Stable (fixed point) |
| `slf` | self-reference | Reference | Stable (no paradox) |
| `rcr` | recursive | Structure | Stable (self-containing) |
| `fls` | false | Truth value | UNSTABLE (liar paradox) |
| `ukn` | unknowable | Epistemic | UNSTABLE |
| `npr` | not processed | Processing | UNSTABLE |

**Recommendation:** Only add stable paradoxes to vocabulary. Unstable ones create genuine logical problems.
